# Germany - Podcast Script

Welcome to the Country Podcast. Today, we explore Germany.

Germany, officially known as the Federal Republic of Germany, sits in the heart of Europe. It stretches from the Baltic Sea and North Sea in the north, down to the Alps in the south. With over eighty-two million people living across sixteen states, Germany is the most populous member of the European Union.

Let us start with geography. Germany shares borders with nine countries. Denmark lies to the north. Poland and the Czech Republic are to the east. Austria and Switzerland sit to the south. And to the west, Germany borders France, Luxembourg, Belgium, and the Netherlands. The capital city is Berlin. The largest urban area is the Ruhr region in the west.

Now let us turn to history.

People have lived in what is now Germany for hundreds of thousands of years. The Neanderthal, an early human species, was discovered in the Neander Valley, which gives us the name we use today.

Around two thousand years ago, Germanic tribes inhabited the region. The Romans tried to conquer these lands, but in the year nine, a Germanic leader named Arminius defeated three Roman legions in the Teutoburg Forest. This battle stopped Roman expansion east of the Rhine River. It is considered one of the most important events in European history.

In the Middle Ages, the Holy Roman Empire emerged. It lasted for nearly a thousand years. In fifteen seventeen, a German monk named Martin Luther started the Protestant Reformation. His translation of the Bible into German helped create a standard German language.

Modern Germany as we know it began in eighteen seventy-one. Prussia, led by Otto von Bismarck, unified the German states into one empire. The German Empire became a major power in Europe.

Then came the First World War. Germany lost. The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh conditions. In nineteen nineteen, the Weimar Republic was born, Germany's first democracy. But economic crisis and political instability followed.

In nineteen thirty-three, Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party took power. They established a totalitarian dictatorship. In nineteen thirty-nine, Germany invaded Poland, starting the Second World War. The war lasted six years. The Nazi regime murdered six million Jews and millions of others in the Holocaust. Germany surrendered in May nineteen forty-five. The country lay in ruins.

After the war, the Allies divided Germany. West Germany became a democracy. East Germany became a communist state under Soviet control. The Berlin Wall, built in nineteen sixty-one, divided Berlin and became a symbol of the Cold War.

In nineteen eighty-nine, the Wall fell. Mass protests in East Germany and changes across Eastern Europe led to the opening of borders. On October third, nineteen ninety, Germany reunified. The two Germanies became one again.

Since reunification, Germany has been a leader in European integration. It is a founding member of the European Union and uses the euro as its currency. Angela Merkel served as chancellor from two thousand five to two thousand twenty-one, the first woman to hold that office. In twenty twenty-five, Friedrich Merz became chancellor.

Now let us look at politics today.

Germany is a federal parliamentary republic. The president, Frank-Walter Steinmeier, serves as head of state with largely ceremonial duties. The chancellor, Friedrich Merz, leads the government. The parliament consists of two houses: the Bundestag, the lower house, and the Bundesrat, the upper house.

Germany has sixteen states, each with its own constitution and government. The political landscape includes several major parties. The Christian Democratic Union and the Social Democratic Party have dominated post-war politics. Smaller parties like the Free Democrats, the Greens, the Left, and the Alternative for Germany also play important roles.

Let us talk about the economy.

Germany has the largest economy in Europe and the third largest in the world by nominal GDP. It is a powerhouse in manufacturing, especially in automobiles, machinery, chemicals, and electronics. Companies like Volkswagen, BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Siemens, and BASF are known worldwide. Germany is also the third largest exporter globally.

The economy faces challenges. Germany is transitioning away from nuclear power and coal toward renewable energy. This transition is called the Energiewende. The country also faces an aging population and the need to integrate millions of refugees and immigrants.

Now for regional context and relations.

Germany is a key player in the European Union. It has close ties with France, historically a rival but now a partner in driving European integration. Relations with Poland have improved greatly since the end of the Cold War. Germany and Austria share a language and much history. Switzerland, while not in the European Union, has strong economic ties with Germany.

Since Russia invaded Ukraine in twenty twenty-two, Germany has supported Ukraine with weapons and humanitarian aid. This marks a significant shift in German foreign policy, as the country had long been cautious about military involvement abroad.

To close, Germany stands at the center of Europe, both geographically and politically. Its history spans from ancient tribes to modern democracy. It has experienced empire, division, dictatorship, and reunification. Today, it is an economic powerhouse and a pillar of the European Union.
